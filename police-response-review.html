OFFICIAL RESPONSE · DOCUMENTATION REVIEW

The current production does not document a reconstructable scene investigation.

The family archive contains a one-page police incident report, dispatch records, limited EMS material, and the 911 call. It does not currently contain scene photographs, a 2021 body-camera recording, a scene diagram, an evidence/property log, a complete EMS device record, a complete OCME file, or a supplemental police narrative documenting a scene investigation.

No body-camera footage from the original death-scene response, complete EMS device package, or complete OCME case file has been located in the family archive.

Documented concern: Without the missing visual, narrative, device, communications, and decision records, later reviewers cannot independently reconstruct what responders observed, who was present, what was moved, what statements were taken, what medical data existed, or how the OCME decision path was reached.

CLASSIFICATION RULE

Expected but unproduced records are logged as authority-side shortcomings

Because these materials would ordinarily be controlled by the responding or reviewing authority—not by the family—this project classifies each expected record that remains absent as an authority-side production or recordkeeping shortcoming until the custodian produces it or provides a written explanation.

Shortcoming

Not in the family archive

The source record has not been located after archive review and is expected to be controlled by Police, Fire/EMS, OCME, or another authority.

Pending explanation

What resolves the classification

Production of the record, or a written statement that it was never created, is no longer retained, was transferred, was destroyed under a retention rule, or is lawfully withheld.

Evidence limit

What the label does not prove

The classification does not itself prove misconduct, intentional concealment, unlawful destruction, or a violation of a particular policy.

WHAT THE LAW ACTUALLY SHOWS

Body-camera absence is not, by itself, a proven 2021 violation

Connecticut’s statewide requirement that sworn officers use body cameras while interacting with the public in their law-enforcement capacity began July 1, 2022—more than seven months after Gina’s death. Therefore, the absence of 2021 footage cannot honestly be labeled a violation of that later statewide mandate.

STATE REQUIREMENTEffective later

Universal body-camera mandate

The statewide universal-use requirement took effect July 1, 2022. The controlling question is whether Waterbury had an earlier departmental policy, whether responding officers were equipped, and what that policy required on November 21, 2021.

Medical-examiner cooperation
