OFFICIAL RESPONSE · DOCUMENTATION REVIEW

The current record does not show a documented scene investigation.

The family archive contains a one-page police incident report, dispatch records, EMS material, and the 911 call. It does not currently contain scene photographs, a 2021 body-camera recording, a scene diagram, an evidence/property log, or a supplemental police narrative documenting a scene investigation.

WHAT IS VERIFIED

The record-production gap

“Not located” means the item has not been found in the records supplied to the family or the current archive. It does not prove that the record never existed.

Located

Official response records

A one-page police incident report, call-summary/dispatch records, EMS material, and the official 911 WAV are preserved in the controlled archive.

Not located

Scene documentation

No November 21, 2021 scene photographs, scene diagram, evidence/property inventory, or supplemental investigative narrative has been located in the current record production.

Not located

Body-camera footage

No body-camera footage from the original death-scene response has been located. A family-created timeline separately references a bodycam used during a 2024 complaint, but that later recording has also not been located or independently verified.

Documented concern: When a death response leaves no located visual scene record and only a minimal police narrative, later reviewers cannot independently reconstruct what officers observed, who was present, what was moved, what statements were made at the scene, or what evidence was considered.

WHAT THE LAW ACTUALLY SHOWS

Body-camera absence is not, by itself, a proven 2021 violation

Connecticut’s statewide requirement that sworn officers use body cameras while interacting with the public in their law-enforcement capacity began July 1, 2022—more than seven months after Gina’s death. Therefore, the absence of 2021 footage cannot honestly be labeled a violation of that later statewide mandate.

STATE REQUIREMENTEffective later

Universal body-camera mandate

The statewide universal-use requirement took effect July 1, 2022. The controlling question is whether Waterbury had an earlier departmental policy, whether responding officers were equipped, and what that policy required on November 21, 2021.

2021 LAWApplicable framework

Medical-examiner cooperation

Connecticut law required law-enforcement personnel to promptly notify and cooperate with the Office of the Chief Medical Examiner for deaths within OCME jurisdiction and to assist in making the body and related evidence available.

UNRESOLVEDPolicy needed

Waterbury’s controlling policy

The current archive does not contain Waterbury Police Department’s body-camera, sudden-death, scene-documentation, or evidence-preservation policies in effect on November 21, 2021. Those policies are required before calling the gap a confirmed departmental violation.

POTENTIAL PROTOCOL LAPSE

The issue requiring an official answer

The defensible finding is not “police definitely violated body-camera law.” It is that the available production is insufficient to demonstrate a thorough, reconstructable scene response.

Question 1Were responding officers equipped with body cameras in November 2021?
Question 2What Waterbury policies governed activation, death scenes, photographs, and supplemental reports?
Question 3Were photographs, recordings, notes, CAD attachments, or evidence logs created but not produced?
Question 4What was communicated to the Office of the Chief Medical Examiner, by whom, and with what instructions?
Question 5Who was present when police arrived, and where are their contemporaneous statements?
Question 6Why does the located police record contain no scene-investigation narrative sufficient for independent review?
