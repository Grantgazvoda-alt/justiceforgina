# Operation Lantern V4 — Authority and Publication Control

## Operating authority

The founder has stated that he is the legally appointed executor of Gina Gazvoda's estate and has approved V4 development with full awareness of the authority, exposure, security, and legal implications.

This repository records the project decision and control framework. It does **not** contain executor certificates, court appointments, identity documents, medical records, credentials, or other sensitive proof. Those materials must remain in a separate restricted legal vault.

## Publication classes

1. **Public verified** — official records or independently verifiable materials approved for publication.
2. **Public family-supplied** — materials supplied by the family and clearly identified as such.
3. **Public redacted medical evidence** — selected records or excerpts approved under executor authority after third-party redaction and relevance review.
4. **Restricted reviewer** — materials available only to authorized attorneys, experts, investigators, or journalists.
5. **Privileged or sealed** — never published or shared outside the legally authorized group.
6. **Disputed or unverified** — preserved internally but not presented as fact.

## Mandatory controls

- No source, no publish.
- Pending charges and allegations are not findings of guilt.
- Remove unrelated patient information, third-party identifiers, account numbers, addresses, signatures, and other unnecessary personal data.
- Maintain an authorization and publication-decision log for each medical or sensitive record.
- Store full medical records outside the public Git repository.
- Public AI answers must cite the exact approved source and must not make independent accusations or medical conclusions.
- Every public correction must preserve a visible change history.
- Founder approval, Evidence review, Security review, and Red Team approval are required before production merge.

## Legal checkpoint

Executor authority is the operating basis for access and controlled disclosure. HIPAA continues to protect a decedent's protected health information for 50 years, and covered entities treat a legally authorized executor or administrator as the decedent's personal representative for information relevant to the representation. Applicable state law, court orders, privilege, contracts, and third-party rights remain separate constraints.
